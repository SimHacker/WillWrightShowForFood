## What Norman has done

Norman Margolus is a Canadian-American physicist and computer scientist (MIT PhD in physics,
1987, under Edward Fredkin), a research affiliate at MIT CSAIL, known for foundational
work on cellular automata and reversible computing. With Tommaso Toffoli he built
the CAM-6 cellular-automata machine and co-wrote *Cellular Automata Machines: A New
Environment for Modeling* (MIT Press, 1987) — the book and hardware that taught a generation
(including Don) to *do* CA. He invented the block cellular automaton and the Margolus
neighborhood, built reversible, universal CA simulations of billiard-ball computers
(the Critters rule), and — with Lev Levitin — proved the Margolus–Levitin theorem,
a fundamental physical speed limit on computation. He helped organize the seminal 1982
Physics & Computation meeting, worked with Tom Knight on the "Flattop" billiard-ball
chip, contributed to early reversible quantum gate logic, and founded the storage company
Permabit. Recurring themes: computronium and programmable matter — computing as
physics, physics as computing.
