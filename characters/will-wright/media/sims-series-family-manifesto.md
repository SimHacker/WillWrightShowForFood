# Sims content creation — family manifesto (archival note)

*From Don Hopkins archival prompts (`big-prompts.txt`, ~2008). Context for the SimFreaks CMS / Original Sims work.*

**Full narration** (Micropolis + Repo Shows + wedding playset + agitprop framing): [**INTRO-NARRATIVE.md**](../../../repo-shows/will-wright/INTRO-NARRATIVE.md)

---

We want to appeal to the Steam Sims 1 audience, a new generation, kids and adults, and especially **families** — kids who grew up playing The Sims and now have families of their own and want to play the new release with their kids.

The most touching story I heard from a fan who used Transmogrifier: a **grandmother** inspired to learn Photoshop and edit XML so she could make personal Sims objects for her grandkids and play together in the game — like Miyamoto's GDC keynote photo of the grandfather with the little granddaughter in his lap, delighted, wielding the pen, while the grandfather watches *her* face, delighted to see his granddaughter enjoying herself.

Dance Dance Revolution and Wii Sports make the **player** the spectacle — friends watch bodies in the real world more than CG on screen.

This is the motivation behind trying to get this done in the sudden window of opportunity: bring a whole new generation to play along with their parents, who might have learned Photoshop and XML from their grandmothers decades ago, and teach The Sims and modern tools (Blender, etc.) to their kids.

The content management system Don was making for Heather first — real life intervened; he was doing it for love not money, and had to take a "real job" to pay rent, so never finished. **Now's the time.**

## SimProv Wedding Playset — rising again

The unreleased **SimProv Wedding Playset** (Don, Heather, Steve, SimBabes, Donna) is the magnum opus in that drawer: Crowd Sitter, Cupid, Buddha, Hope Chest, premium craft objects — composable tools for staging a wedding together. It was built when marriage equality was still a fight in the real world. Republishing it for the Steam Sims 1 generation — families who grew up on The Sims and now play with their kids — is deliberate: proof that inclusive family play was always the point.
