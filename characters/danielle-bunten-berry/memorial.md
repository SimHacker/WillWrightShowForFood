# In memoriam — Danielle Bunten Berry (1949–1998)

*Memorial mode: we represent and discuss her documented work and the stories friends tell; we do not
interview her or speak as her, and we invent no quotes or inner life.*
[Memorial standards](../../schemas/portrayal-standards.yml#memorial_mode)

Danielle Bunten Berry believed, long before the industry caught up, that the best thing a game could
do was **bring people together in the same room**. At Ozark Softscape she designed **M.U.L.E.**
(1983) — a four-player game of trading, cooperation, and cutthroat economics that became one of
Electronic Arts' first and most cherished titles — and **The Seven Cities of Gold** (1984), an
open-world exploration game that helped define the genre. Across *Cytron Masters*, *Modem Wars*,
*Global Conquest*, and *Robot Rascals*, she kept returning to the same idea: play is social, and
software should serve human connection, not replace it.

She is widely remembered for the sentiment that no one ever reached the end of their life wishing
they had spent more time alone with their computer. It was the through-line of her design
philosophy, and it still reads as a challenge to everyone who builds games and tools today.

**The Sims was dedicated to her** — Will Wright's tribute to a friend whose people-first, play-
together values run straight through The Sims, multiplayer SimCity, and this whole project. We
remember her best by inviting the people who knew her — above all **[Will Wright](../will-wright/README.md)**
— to tell the real stories.

We celebrate her documented public work; we do not speak as her.

*This is a collaborative memorial, not a signed work. Those who knew Danielle — family, friends, and
colleagues — are warmly invited to edit, correct, and add their own memories, documents, and stories
whenever they're ready.*
