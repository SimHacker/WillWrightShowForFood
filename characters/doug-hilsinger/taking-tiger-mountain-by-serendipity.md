# Taking Tiger Mountain, by Serendipity 🐅🏔️

*A true story Don loves to tell — and would love to tell properly **with** Doug, Brian, Scott, and
Will. Album facts are public record (sources below); the San Francisco encounter is Don's own
recollection.* [Portrayal standards](../../schemas/portrayal-standards.yml)

## The record

In Fall 2003, San Francisco multi-instrumentalist **Doug Hilsinger** did something quietly
audacious: he re-recorded **Brian Eno's *Taking Tiger Mountain (By Strategy)*** (1974) — the whole
album, track for track — with vocalist **Caroleen Beatty**. The self-imposed rule was the soul of
it: **no keyboards, no synthesizers, no drum machines.** Hilsinger played *everything* by hand —
drums, bass, guitar, pedal steel, sitar guitar, glockenspiel, melodica, autoharp, percussion,
treatments — and Beatty sang. The result turned Eno's famously **chilly, machine-tinged** 1974
experiment into something **warm, organic, and aggressively alive**: the same arrangements, a
human pulse, a woman's voice where there'd been Eno's.

They made it **for themselves, for love** — it was never meant for stores. Then, at one of
**Brian Eno's Long Now Foundation** appearances in San Francisco, they slipped him a CD-R of their
secret love letter. Eno's reply became the album's blessing:

> *"I am deeply moved by your versions of my songs. I like it very, very much."* — Brian Eno

…and, drily, to Pitchfork: *"They aren't bad songs after all."* That response brought the tribute
out of the bedroom and into the world — released **2004 on DBK Works (CD #111)**, the album's
**30th anniversary**.

## The serendipity

Don's path crossed Doug's by pure coincidence, in San Francisco, around the time of the **2006 Long
Now talk where Eno and Will Wright shared a stage**. Don walked into a bar and heard music that was
**strangely familiar yet completely new** — like hearing *Taking Tiger Mountain* through someone
else's ears and mind. He didn't quite place it, so he asked the bartender about the song… and the
bartender turned out to be **Doug himself** — one of Eno's biggest fans, who had **performed the
very record that was playing.** Too many coincidences to chalk up to chance. Doug gave Don a copy
of the CD on the spot. Don has treasured it — and the story — ever since.

## Why it belongs in the show

This is the **living proof of a principle** that runs through the whole Repo Show idea: you honor
something not by copying it, but by **transforming** it. Doug took a cold, brilliant 30-year-old
machine-music album and re-grew it by hand, warm and human — and the original's *author* loved it.
That's exactly the move a Repo Show makes with old code and old ideas: revive, reinterpret, add a
pulse, and let the lineage continue.

It's a perfect thread to pull **with Brian, Scott, and Will** on a generative-systems show:

- **Constraints create freedom** — the "no keyboards" rule is the most Eno-ish (and most
  Oblique-Strategy) thing imaginable, and it's what gives the cover its character.
- **Transformation as homage** — the recursive move: original → tribute → whatever we make next.
- **Scenius** — a Bay Area scene (Waycross, the ENORCHESTRA, Caroleen's Mekons/Jon Langford
  connections) doing it for love, not money.

## Don's wish
