## Opening — models, simulation, game, and interface design {#opening-framework}

**[1:37] Will Wright:** Thank you, Terry. Yeah, I guess I'm kind of from that in-between generation. I spent a lot of time building models when I was a kid. Way too much time building models when I was a kid. And I really was fascinated by the idea of these kind of micro systems, models, micro worlds, whatever. You go out there now, and you can't find models the way they used to be. Used to be you could find plastic models all over the place. And that's what kids did, especially boys. Whereas girls would play with the dolls, which was a different kind of modeling — more of a social system model. That whole industry seems to have been displaced recently by the video game industry, which to me is kind of sad in a way, because on the modeling front there was so much creativity involved.

**[2:34]** I started out with these plastic models you'd glue together. Then I got into the pre-formed wooden ones, building airplanes and putting paper over it. And then eventually started building my own just out of scratch wood. And it was really interesting to me, because you could get such a good sense of the structural definition of a system with these models. So when I got a computer in my early 20s — a friend of mine talked me into buying one — I very quickly realized how cool the computer was as a modeling tool. The computer all of a sudden allows you to start modeling *process* in addition to just structure. So my definitions here are kind of loose and freewheeling, but like a tree, the structure might be the leaves and branches, process might be combustion, structural change in the tree. So for the first time with the computer I had a tool that I could start modeling process with. And as a model builder I just went hog wild and taught myself to program, and got into the games industry. This is about 15 years ago.

**[3:36]** Now I was supposed to talk here today about interface design, and I'm going to try to. But really I'm going to touch on three major areas: simulation design, game design, and interface design. Because those three to me are very tightly linked. I can't just design the interface after the game's done, or design the game in isolation from the interface. Each one of these supports the other, and a lot of times there'll be a game I want to do, but I can't design the interface to support it, or the simulation underneath that it needs to have. So to me these three go in this dynamic process and design together.

**[4:16]** I'm going to show you some of our older games, in chronological order — going through some of the issues we were thinking about, why we did things the way we did, and then lessons learned after it was out there, and hundreds of thousands of people were complaining about it and telling us how bad a job we did. We'd get more perspective after time. And then each one after that we would try to address the problems, and usually we'd overshoot, or go off in a totally different direction and invent new problems for the users.

**[4:47]** On the simulation side, most of the games we do are about real-world systems. So the simulation in a sense is leading the charge on the educational front. We pick a system that we want to educate the user about, then we look at how much of the system do we want to wrap the user's mind around. There are a lot of issues in terms of how high is the fidelity of the simulation — when does it match reality versus user expectations? On the game design, a lot of times we will decide to make the simulation match user expectations rather than reality, if otherwise it would destroy the gameplay.

**[5:32]** Games are really all about problem solving, constraints, challenges. Simulation is more about modeling the real world — so the simulation is where the problem is usually embedded. As far as the simulation is concerned they're not problems, it's just data going through some algorithms. It's the game design that basically points you toward a goal — what is a problem, what constraints the user has to deal with, and those constraints are typically where the gameplay comes from.

**[6:14]** The interface has to support both of these processes. You have to visualize the simulation. There are a lot of issues with how much of the simulation we make explicit to the user. Sometimes we don't want to show every last detail — we want to prop up this illusion of reality, and that's one of these issues where the game design is in opposition to the simulation design sometimes. As an educator, a lot of people would rather see simulations that were very explicit in how they modeled. They want users to know exactly how SimCity is calculating the traffic rate, or why crime appears. On a game design front, that's not always desirable. If you make the system too explicit, A: you destroy that illusion of reality, and B: you take away some of the challenge of the problem solving.

**[7:12]** One of the things we think about on the education front is that when we're designing a model we're not necessarily designing a computer model. **Our real end product is the mental model in the player's head.** We're trying to give them maybe a more robust model of the way a city works, or a planet works. The computer model really is just an incremental step in that direction. **It's like a compiler for the mental model.**

**[8:10]** One of the paradigms we've tried to use is to think of them a little bit more as toys rather than games — to leave them more open-ended. Most games you have a single-minded goal, you solve it, you win. The simulations we've been moving towards are more open-ended, where the user is deciding what the goal is before they play. When they play SimCity, we don't tell them you have to get the biggest city. People take it and play different ways: "what kind of city do I want to build? I want the happiest possible residents." So it's a little more open-ended, the way a ball can be used for different games. The user decides what the goal is.

**[9:10]** There was a really cool short story written by Stanisław Lem called the Seventh Sally. He's got these robot cultures, and one of the robots, an inventor named Trurl, came across this asteroid where there was a deposed dictator, all by himself, really sad because he didn't have a kingdom to rule. So Trurl invented this magical invention — a box with a whole kingdom inside that he could rule and enslave and do bad things to, with little knobs to control them. He never got real explicit about what the interface was.
**Don Hopkins:** Really fancy knobs!
**Will Wright:** Yeah, really fancy knobs. But he could go down to the point of issuing decrees, making that guy slip off his horse in the midst of a battle. The idea of it is kind of what's inspired me through the years to build these things. We're nowhere near building Trurl's invention, but that's kind of why I do a lot of this.

## SimEarth — live demo and postmortem {#simearth-demo}

**[10:08] (SimEarth demo)** This project was a little bit ambitious — a simulation of the planet. The intent was to show how complex planetary systems are, the linkages between systems most people don't think about. People have been doing climate models, or evolution models, in isolation, without regard for the way these things link together — so one attempt was to show the inter-relationship between these different fields. Another was to give a sense of the vast time scales over which planets evolve. Our task was to figure out: what would the interface to a planet be?

*[SimEarth walkthrough: global vs. local windows; "this distinction applies to the controls and the displays"; making it look like a toy planet vs. a simulation; mapping onto a sphere in real time was "more of a macho programmer thing… a horrendous amount of code… probably was not worth it. But live and learn." Terrain, continental drift, earthquakes, ocean temperatures, currents, biomes; small keys; information density "like really fancy car stereos with the billion and a half buttons" — fun in the showroom, but "you get totally lost with all the buttons."]*

**[20:25]** This game runs at four time scales: Geologic, Evolution, Civilized, Technology — to show how changes on Earth accelerate over time. *[The bottom-row icons are ordered from longest time scales on the left to shortest on the right; the simulation itself changes depending on the time scale.]*

**[23:21] (the Gaia face)** This is our first stupid attempt at an agent-based interface. **Basically this product grew through accretion.** We started with a simple climate model, "oh that's cool, let's put in a continental drift model," "let's put in an evolution model," "a civilization model." As designers it made sense — we were layering this model, everything resting on layers below it. But we showed this to new users looking to buy some quick entertainment, and they were quite overwhelmed. I got a paper from somebody doing group-learning studies — **they proved it took five people to play SimEarth.** One person just couldn't divide up the conceptual things and make the linkages. So the Gaia face was our last-ditch attempt to collapse all the data into one score, give the user some sense of how they were doing. *[Turn off the Sun — "Doesn't like that!" — planet freezes.]*

**[25:21]** The biggest failure of this had to do with the failure mode itself. If you're dealing with conflicts and challenges, **the whole thing is about failure.** You want the user to be failing, continuously, in an entertaining way — confront problems, fail several times, **but know why they failed.** Even in Doom: I went around that corner, I got blasted, I know why. In SimEarth, people were failing and had no idea why. And as designers, we had no idea why either. The simulation was just too complex — chaotic attractors in the attraction space. So that was almost a failure of the simulation design.

## SimAnt — live demo and postmortem {#simant-demo}

**[27:05] (SimAnt demo)** We decided SimEarth was too complex, so we swung the other extreme: just simulate an ant colony. Local window (the ant) and global window. The primary tool in this game is the ant. *[We had planned a 3D nest display, but prototypes baffled people, so we fell back on users' real experience — the 2D side-view ant farm, and straight-down view outside. People didn't even notice the viewpoint shift going out of the hole.]* You crawl around, look for food, feed your colony, establish food trails, fight the red colony for the patch. There's a meta-game: take over the yard like Go; the real reward is getting into the house, around the fridge and sink.

**[33:00]** We kept the failure modes very direct and obvious — walk by a spider, get slurped up; you know why. We made death free: when you die you come back as another ant. **The ants are like the cells, the colony is like the organism.** *[Triangular caste control; question-mark help; pheromone displays.]* One of the main things we were trying to communicate is **why ants are so cool** — little things operating on local rules, no idea what's going on, but add them up and you get the surprisingly intelligent colony. *[The larva-sorting rule discovered through simulation: pick up a larva if you haven't smelled its smell nearby; put it down where the smell matches — and they sort themselves efficiently.]* We wanted users to **pull** the information out of the system rather than have it pushed at them. *[The "mystery button" does something random — kids loved the "let's get crazy" button; adults didn't relate.]* Our failure here was more on the marketing side — we targeted the wrong demographic; 10-year-olds loved it, 20-and-up thought it too simplistic.

## SimCity 2000 — live demo and postmortem {#simcity2000-demo}

**[42:11] (SimCity 2000 demo)** Rather than two windows, we collapsed everything into one window, and added zoom. Isometric perspective, so we added rotate buttons. *[The centering tool — "I've always had a problem with scroll bars."]* Floating palettes you bring up when needed. We tried to collapse as much information as possible into the main window, make everything graphic in a way that made sense, matched reality. Tools are point-select (drop a tree), line-select (road), or area-select (zones); each works as a single click. We organized the tools in the same order, with similar icons, to the old SimCity, so players could transfer. Extra detail went into pop-up menus — easy learning ramp. Signs let you name districts and buildings: prop up the illusion of a real city, and get the user to care about it.

**[48:05]** SimCity works better than the ant colony or the planet because **the user has a lot more creative control, and permanence.** In SimEarth, no matter what I do, it's erased in a billion years of erosion. In SimCity, I build a road and it stays. After playing a while, what's on screen is something I've created. *[Data-layer map; population/education cohort graphs; national economic model — steel, textiles, petrochemical, electronics by era; build schools/museums/libraries to raise the workforce EQ. Underground infrastructure. The newspaper — a "Mad Libs" output of the simulation; most people burned out on it after about two weeks.]*

## The Calvin Syndrome {#calvin-syndrome}

**[53:48] (the Calvin Syndrome)** When we showed the original SimCity, the first thing people did was find the bulldozer and blow something up — "hey that's cool" — then attack the city. Like poking an ant colony with a stick to see what happens. They had to perturb the system to see: is it fragile? Is this a painting, or is it really a simulation? Kids and adults, almost universal. So we named that the **Calvin Syndrome**, and that's when we put in the disaster menu — tornado, earthquake, monster, or everything all at once. People like seeing the thing go down in ruins — Nero on the fiddle. But it's funny: people have to see how fragile a system is before they really appreciate building it. They'd get the destruction out of their system first, then start rebuilding, invest 10–20 hours, and really empathize — when an earthquake hit, they'd say "**I've** been hit by an earthquake," not "my city's been hit."

## SimEarth too hard, SimAnt too easy, SimCity 2000 just right {#three-games-wrap}

**[57:21]** SimEarth was too hard, SimAnt too easy, **SimCity 2000 was just right.** The only real criticism was from educators who wanted the model more explicit — the algorithms for crime, traffic, pollution. That maybe needs a different design.

We've avoided some costly blunders that way. But most value comes from informal testing — showing new people. I've got a nine-year-old daughter, I show her all the time, her friends, adults. I look at the spectrum between kids and adults, how they relate to it. We learned that lesson on SimAnt — we might have had a fun game, but if we target the wrong demographic, it might not be the success it could be.

**[1:01:46] Student:** You say "we" — I'm interested in how you work with your colleagues, how you set up the group. Do you have an ant expert? What's the team like?
**Will Wright:** Generally on all these products I've done the research, because that's the part I really enjoy. Most of these products I wrote about half the code, so I was heavily involved in all the details. On SimAnt, SimEarth, and SimCity it was me and one other person in very close collaboration — same person for SimEarth and SimCity. Most of it was through discussion: I'd come up with an idea, he'd tell me what a terrible idea it was, he'd come up with an idea, we'd end up with some compromise in between. The current games involve bigger teams, three to five people. But depending on whose project it is, **there has to be one person with the X on their back that's going to call the shots.** You might have a group discussion over "is this icon blue or red?" or "is this game about ants or planets?", but some person has got to be pulling that thing through. **I've never seen a product design through a committee that came out well.** You really have to have one vision behind it. And it's really hard to communicate that vision — I have this idea in the back of my head about why it would be so cool to have a simulation of a thunderstorm, and people stare at me with a blank stare. I just cannot convey that to the point where they're going to drop a half million dollars on it. Luckily I'm at the point where I can push it through — they'll trust me on it. On the financial side, people want control, predictability; they don't know what you're talking about. But you all have to have one person with that idea, all the way through, from form to style to mechanics to plumbing.

**[1:04:42] Student:** Who are your customers — demographics and other characteristics, the people who buy it initially and the committed users?

In focus groups we've had avid gamers 60–65 years old. In the game field there are almost three major areas: the kids' early-education market (purchase decision is parent-based); "Nintendo Gulch," about 10–16 (the kids drive the purchase, Hack-And-Slash Three); and the adult hardcore gamer market. Across all that, in a broad thin veneer, is the PC gaming market — we have 18-year-olds, 15-year-olds who aren't into Hack-And-Slash, buying and playing SimCity.

**[1:06:46] Student:** What do you know about those users besides age and sex — what distinguishes the person who gets this and uses it?

The Nintendo industry has gone in and decimated the model-building, hobby, toy industry. We ask buyers what other games they have and look at intersections — but our games have a much broader demographic than most. We have a lot of players who only play two or three games, and this is one of them.

## Data portability and the hobby model {#data-portability}

Most of the game industry right now is built on kind of the movie model: you spend a lot of money developing one big title, you come out with it, you advertise it, either it goes or it dies, and then you do the next one separate. Except for sequels — the one genre that does better than any other. What we've tried to do, slowly over time, is build our games more as a **hobby model**, where people buy and collect things that relate to the last things they collected. It's like a train set: some people get into building the hills and trees, they could care less about the train; others get into the village, or the track switching. Everybody comes in, takes their slant, focuses in great detail. I'd like the game industry to evolve that way — **I want the games to actually be able to have persistent data that can move from one game to another.** Let me show you something real quick along those lines.

## Dollhouse demo {#dollhouse-demo}

**[1:10:02] (Dollhouse demo)** This is one of the things I'm working on, by the way. This is a game I call **Dollhouse.** And if this looks familiar, it's because **I've just loaded a SimCity file into here.** So what we're seeing is a SimCity file, but now I can zoom down to the street level. This little area by the street is what I'm zooming into. I can keep zooming in, and now I can get a little person. **This is me.** I'm actually controlling this person like a puppet — I can wave, and walk around. I can walk anywhere in the city. We don't have a full database in this, but I have a database for all the roads and terrain. It's very feasible to put a database in for every building, so I could walk into any building.

**[1:10:58]** The tools in this game are more architectural. I'll build a foundation here — this is a wall tool, I have different wall styles. We're designing kind of like a little CAD program that a 10-year-old could use, like you'd build a little dollhouse. We also have landscaping tools — planting my yard; we played with L-systems for plants in real time, a little too slow. These are windows. I can zoom into the house. This isn't much of a defined game — it's almost more of what I'd consider a hobby. I could take a city I built in SimCity and now come live in it. I can stake a claim, build my house, decorate it, and invite my friends over. We're looking at having a little family model inside, with little personality models for these guys. There's behavior embedded in some of these objects.

**[1:12:19]** So I click on an object. *(Puts down toilet — laughter! Sim sits down on toilet — more laughter!)* Now what's interesting here is that that person, in the person's data structure, **there's no knowledge of any objects in this environment whatsoever.** The object itself contains the descriptions of how a person interacts with it, and why, what the animation sequence would be, and the scheduling. So this is my little avatar. I can also pop in some simulated people that walk around. They're kind of ghosts — they don't know about the walls. They'll have conversations, some will panhandle each other.

**[1:13:02]** What's cool is that the behavior is entirely distributed in the environment. So a person's in a room, they have certain motivations, needs — hungry, sleepy, lonely, angry. They scan the room for people and objects, and **the objects are all kind of advertising: "If you're angry, pick up me and throw me!", "If you're hungry, eat me!"** And there's a communication there. It's all data driven. Even the animations — component-driven geometry. So they don't have to know how to ride a bike, or sit on the toilet. The object tells them how to: "okay, you're gonna make me less hungry, I'm gonna interact with you, what do I do?" The object tells it what to do. The cool thing is we can drop new objects in later — maybe network-based. We can even have tools where the users build these objects — designing furniture, exercise equipment — then post them on the net, and other people download them, and the environment gets more rich. And so to me, this is what I want to move towards: the hobbyist thing, distributed environment, with objects that can move from one game to another. This is actually a very similar data structure to Doom — I could be in here in a 3D point of view, shooting the person on the toilet if I wanted to. Maybe it's a different game player in a different game, but still running off the same server. So in a way you can think of this as a graphic MUD.

## Simulating people {#people-simulation}

**[1:14:25] Student:** What about from person to person — the information contained within the objects; there can be information in another person that you want to interact with. Have you looked at reasons why you'd want to do that?
**Will Wright:** Oh yeah, that's the hard problem. Simulating ants is hard enough; when you get to people there's really no hope. There are two issues. As a multiplayer network MUD, you might have a thousand people playing SimCity from the bottom up, each building their own house — then that issue is less important, because most people are real, you're dealing with puppets. As a standalone game (our closer target), we have to deal with how to do people-to-people. The best thing we can do is **prop up a convincing illusion.** We don't have to do a valid simulation of human personality — we put up something ambiguous enough that somebody can read in what they want. Right now people come up and converse, but you don't hear what they're saying — they gesture, sometimes look mad, sometimes contemplative. It's interesting how much people read into that.

**[1:16:14]** This is a dynamic we've seen again and again: something happens in SimCity and they say "I was running my nuclear reactor near the red line, there was so much smoke, this plane crashed, and because of that this happened" — a long causal chain I *know* does not exist. I designed the simulation; there's no linkage between the power plant and the planes crashing. But they're convinced it exists.
**Don Hopkins:** They're using it as a medium to tell stories about.
**Will Wright:** Yeah!
**Don Hopkins:** Where they're using it as a piece of paper, to write.
**Will Wright:** Yeah, exactly right. There's a parallel simulation going on in the game. Everybody's taking a linear path through this, and most people will attempt to understand things like this with a story — "I did this, then that happened, because of that" — so the story becomes their logical reverse-engineering of the simulation they're playing inside of. On the people's side, if this is a dollhouse, we don't want the dolls to be sentient — we want them interesting enough that I can play games with them.

**[1:17:29]** There was a really interesting doll — Worlds of Wonder, the Julie doll, a $250 doll with voice recognition. It was the only doll I've ever seen that appealed to grown men — a hacker's doll. But they put it in focus groups with girls, who played with it for a while, then took the batteries out and kept playing. The girls were propping up an elaborate fantasy, and the doll was supposed to be a *structure* for that fantasy, not the fantasy itself — the doll was telling them what the fantasy was, conflicting with what the girls were saying, interfering with their play. So we can take that path with these people — deal with them at a very local state-machine, Braitenberg-machine level: angry, hungry, sleepy. And maybe their conversation has structural ambiguity, more like Peanuts — you hear the adults "mwa mwa mwa," you can tell if they're mad or angry, but you read into it. As a commercial company we have to ship a game, we're not doing a research project.

**[1:19:35] Student:** If people can read into what the characters are doing, won't that interfere with their expectations if the simulation doesn't meet them?
**Will Wright:** Yeah, it comes down to what the simulation is — addressed on the game-design side. I don't think there's any way I'm going to come close to simulating a person. With a lot of tricks, I can have these people walking through the day — getting up, taking a shower, fixing breakfast, going to work, deciding "I'm bored, I'll sit on the couch and turn on the TV." How you make that into a game design might have a lot to do with how valid the simulation has to be. If it's a story game where drama has to unfold at the right time, then the model would need to be a real simulation. I really don't think we're anywhere near that — unless you're in a very tightly confined domain, you'll have a hard time with open-ended simulation about people. So we constrain what the user is doing. Maybe it's just a dollhouse — I get two people to meet at a party, everything else is indeterminate, and I'm moving furniture around.

## Multiplayer vs AI {#multiplayer-vs-ai}

**Don Hopkins:** You're saying a multi-user game would be easier to design than an AI game, because you can use other people.
**Will Wright:** Oh yeah, far easier, I think so.
**Don Hopkins:** It's just the technology of communicating and time lags.
**Will Wright:** You're always building models of the system you're playing with. It's easy to build a model of a stupid computer agent. It's hard to build a model of your head while I'm talking to you. That's what's interesting — trying to reverse-engineer your thought process.
**Don Hopkins:** There are a lot of commercial services like WorldsAway and Habitat that only have other people, no robots, and they're pretty successful just because of that.
**Will Wright:** Yeah, people are pretty interesting.

**[1:22:08] Student:** Your games seem the most likely of any to have a cooperative multiplayer mode — you mentioned five people playing SimEarth. Does that happen much?
**Will Wright:** Actually it happens a lot, especially with kids. My daughter almost never plays by herself, but if a friend's over they'll both play — one at the controls, the other the backseat driver, and it's the dialogue between the two that makes it interesting. **The game is facilitating a social interaction.** That's a big attraction of the Nintendo Gulch peer group: I get better at Super Mario so I can tell Tommy; it's social currency. Our games make that easier because of the time scale — not like Doom where every second I'm moving; ours are more like a leisurely stroll.

## Industry surprises {#industry-surprises}

**[1:23:54] Student:** Looking back 10 or 15 years, what have been some of the surprises in how things have gone in the industry?
**Will Wright:** I'm surprised there seems to be a shift away from deeper games — this happened with Doom (which I've played enough that I can't criticize it for not being fun; it's a very different thing than SimCity).
